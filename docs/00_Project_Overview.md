## 1. The Problem in Healthcare Data

Healthcare generates more data per person than almost any other domain. Every time a patient
visits a hospital, a cascade of structured records is created:

- A patient record (demographics, insurance, language, marital status)
- An encounter record (what kind of visit, where, how long, why)
- Observation records (every measurement: blood pressure, blood glucose, body weight, lab tests)
- Condition records (every diagnosis: hypertension, diabetes, asthma)
- Medication records (every prescription: what drug, what dose, for how long)

A single patient with a chronic condition like diabetes may accumulate thousands of records
over their lifetime — each one linked to the others in a web of relationships.

This data is extraordinarily valuable. It contains the answers to questions like:

- Which combination of medications is most effective for elderly patients with both
  Type 2 diabetes and hypertension?
- Can we predict a patient's risk of hospital readmission within 30 days from their
  clinical history?
- What is the real-world prevalence of a rare genetic disorder across different ethnic groups?

**The problem is that this data cannot be easily accessed by the people who need it most.**

Researchers, data scientists, AI engineers, medical students, and public health analysts
are routinely blocked from accessing real patient data — not because the data does not exist,
but because the legal, ethical, and technical barriers to sharing it are enormous.

## 2. Why Healthcare Data Cannot Simply Be Shared

Healthcare records are some of the most sensitive information a person can have. Leaked
medical records have been used to discriminate against people in employment, insurance, and
personal relationships. In 2015, the US Department of Health and Human Services reported that
healthcare data breaches had exposed over 113 million patient records in a single year.

Several laws make it illegal to share patient data without strict controls:

| Law | Region | What it protects |
|---|---|---|
| HIPAA (Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act) | United States | Patient health information |
| GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation) | European Union | All personal data, including health |
| Data Protection Act 2018 | United Kingdom | Personal data, explicit rules for health data |
| Personal Data Protection Act (PDPA) | Singapore, Thailand, others | Personally identifiable information |

Beyond law, there is an ethical obligation. Patients share their most private information
with healthcare providers under an expectation of confidentiality. Violating that trust —
even for research with good intentions — can cause lasting harm.

**This creates a paradox:**  
The data that would most improve healthcare outcomes is locked away from the researchers
who could use it to improve healthcare outcomes.

## 3. Types of Healthcare Data: A Spectrum of Risk

Not all approaches to sharing healthcare data carry the same risk. Understanding the
differences is crucial to understanding why synthetic data is a fundamentally different
(and safer) approach.

```
High Risk ←──────────────────────────────────────────→ Low Risk

Real Data    →    Anonymised    →    De-identified    →    Synthetic
```

### Real Data

The original patient records exactly as generated by the hospital's electronic health
record system. Contains every piece of information — names, dates of birth, addresses,
Social Security numbers, diagnoses, medications, lab results.

**Risk:** If leaked, a real person is directly harmed. Even without names, combinations
of attributes (age + zip code + rare diagnosis) can uniquely identify individuals.
Research has shown that 87% of Americans can be uniquely identified using only their
date of birth, gender, and zip code.

### Anonymised Data

Real patient data where obvious identifiers (names, SSN, phone numbers) have been
removed. The data structure and values are otherwise unchanged.

**Risk:** Anonymisation is reversible. The famous Netflix Prize dataset was "anonymised"
by removing usernames, but researchers were able to re-identify individuals by cross-
referencing with public IMDB reviews. Healthcare data is far richer than movie ratings
and correspondingly easier to re-identify.

### De-identified Data

Real patient data processed to meet a specific legal standard (e.g., the HIPAA Safe
Harbor method requires removing 18 specific types of identifiers and ensuring no small
cell sizes). More rigorous than anonymisation, but still derived from real patient records.

**Risk:** De-identification techniques can fail. The HIPAA Safe Harbor standard was
designed in 1996 — before the era of social media, genomic databases, and large-scale
data linkage. Modern re-identification attacks can compromise de-identified records
using external data sources.

**Crucially: de-identified data still originated from real people.** Even if you cannot
trace a record back to its source, that source person exists.

### Synthetic Data

Statistically generated records that match the distributions of real data but were not
derived from any specific real person. No real patient's information was used to create
any specific synthetic record.

**Risk:** Theoretically zero re-identification risk, because there is no real person
behind any synthetic record. The risk that remains is the risk of statistical disclosure
— if the model memorised a rare training example, a synthetic record might closely
resemble a real one. This risk is quantifiable and can be measured (Phase 5 of this
project does exactly this).

## 4. What Synthetic Healthcare Data Is

Synthetic healthcare data is patient data that was generated by a computer model
rather than collected from real patients.

The key word is "statistically faithful." A good synthetic dataset:

- Has the same distribution of ages, genders, and ethnicities as the real population
- Preserves the same prevalence rates for diseases (e.g., if 12% of real patients have
  diabetes, approximately 12% of synthetic patients should too)
- Maintains the same correlations between variables (e.g., hypertension is more common
  in older patients in the real data, and should be more common in older synthetic patients too)
- Has the same temporal patterns (e.g., the time between follow-up visits for chronic
  conditions follows a similar distribution)
- Preserves foreign key relationships (every synthetic encounter belongs to exactly one
  synthetic patient)

**What synthetic data is NOT:**

- It is not real patient data with values changed randomly (that would be data corruption)
- It is not real patient data with values swapped between patients (that would be data shuffling)
- It is not fake data made up by a programmer (that would not reflect real clinical patterns)
- It is not a summary of the real data (that would be statistics, not a dataset)

Synthetic data is generated by a machine learning model that has learned the underlying
statistical structure of the real data, and then samples new records from that learned
structure — just as a language model learns to generate realistic sentences without copying
any specific sentence from its training data.

## 5. Why Synthetic Data Matters — Real-World Applications

### Hospitals and Healthcare Systems

A hospital IT department wants to test a new patient scheduling system. Testing it with
real patient records would require data governance approval, legal review, and patient
consent. Testing it with synthetic records that have the same demographic mix and
appointment patterns requires none of these — and the test results are equally useful.

### Medical Research

A researcher studying the long-term cardiovascular outcomes of a new diabetes medication
needs a patient cohort spanning 10 years. Real-world data may not be available due to
consent restrictions or may require months of ethics board approvals. Synthetic data can
be generated instantly and iteratively refined.

### Drug Discovery

Pharmaceutical companies use predictive models to identify candidate compounds. Training
these models requires large, diverse patient datasets. Synthetic data can fill in
demographic gaps where real data is sparse — for example, rare diseases or underrepresented
ethnic groups.

### AI Model Development

Machine learning engineers building clinical decision support tools need labelled training
data. Real-world clinical data often has labelling inconsistencies, missing values, and
class imbalances. Synthetic data can be generated with controlled properties — more balanced
class distributions, controlled missing value rates — while still reflecting real patterns.

### Medical Education

Medical students learning to diagnose from patient histories need access to a wide range
of clinical presentations. Real patient data cannot be used for teaching without patient
consent. Synthetic patient records with realistic clinical narratives can be used freely.

### Government and Public Health

Public health modellers simulating the spread of an infectious disease need realistic
synthetic populations — age distributions, household structures, comorbidity rates — to
make their models accurate. Synthetic health records are the input.

### Insurance and Actuarial Science

Actuaries pricing health insurance products need large datasets with mortality rates,
hospitalisation frequencies, and drug cost distributions. Synthetic data allows
development and validation of pricing models without regulatory approval cycles.

## 6. Why This Project Exists — The Gap We Are Solving

Public synthetic health data generators already exist. The most well-known is Synthea,
which is used in this project as the source data. Synthea generates realistic FHIR
(Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources) bundles using rule-based clinical models.

However, Synthea has a fundamental limitation: **it is a rule-based simulator, not a
data-driven model.** It generates patient histories according to pre-programmed clinical
pathways. The data it produces reflects the rules its developers encoded, not the
statistical patterns found in a real patient population.

**SynthFHIR addresses this by treating Synthea as a data source, not the final product.**

Instead of using Synthea data directly, SynthFHIR:

1. Uses Synthea to generate a realistic initial population (1,000 patients, FHIR format)
2. Parses the FHIR bundles into structured relational tables
3. Applies clinical preprocessing to prepare the data for machine learning
4. Trains deep generative models (CTGAN, PAR) that learn the statistical structure of
   the Synthea population
5. Generates a new synthetic population whose statistics are derived from learned
   distributions, not from pre-programmed rules
6. Evaluates the generated population for statistical fidelity (utility) and privacy

This approach means that if SynthFHIR were pointed at a real hospital's EHR data instead
of Synthea data, it would generate synthetic data that reflects that specific hospital's
patient population — without exposing any real patient records.

**The gap we are solving:** A production-grade, open-source, FHIR-native synthetic health
data generation pipeline that:

- Accepts FHIR JSON input (the healthcare industry standard format)
- Preserves relational structure across multiple linked tables
- Uses state-of-the-art generative models (not rule-based simulation)
- Is fault-tolerant for long-running training on cloud platforms (Kaggle, Colab)
- Produces output in both tabular (CSV) and FHIR (JSON) formats
- Quantifies both utility (how realistic is the synthetic data?) and
  privacy (could a real patient be re-identified from the synthetic data?)

## 7. Project Goals and Scope

### In Scope

- Parse 1,000 Synthea-generated FHIR JSON bundles
- Build five linked relational tables (patients, encounters, observations, conditions, medications)
- Apply clinical preprocessing: temporal encoding, PII removal, outlier handling
- Train CTGAN models for cross-sectional tables and PAR models for sequential tables
- Generate synthetic versions of all five tables preserving relational integrity
- Evaluate statistical fidelity using column-shape tests, correlation matrices, and ML efficacy
- Evaluate privacy using singling-out, linkability, and inference attack metrics
- Reconstruct synthetic records back into valid FHIR JSON bundles
- Build an interactive dashboard comparing real and synthetic data distributions

### Out of Scope

- Real patient data ingestion (Synthea is the data source)
- Clinical NLP (free-text clinical notes)
- Image data (radiology, pathology)
- Real-time or streaming data
- Production deployment into a hospital EHR system

## 9. Project Phases at a Glance

```
Phase 1: Data Generation
  └─ Synthea generates 1,000 FHIR JSON bundles
       Input: Synthea configuration
       Output: data/raw/*.json (1,000 files)

Phase 2: FHIR Parsing
  └─ Extract 5 structured tables from FHIR bundles
       Input: data/raw/*.json
       Output: data/processed/{patients,encounters,observations,conditions,medications}.csv

Phase 3: Clinical Preprocessing
  └─ Feature engineering + SDV metadata generation
       Input: data/processed/*.csv
       Output: data/ready/*_ready.csv + data/ready/metadata.json

Phase 4A: Smoke Test
  └─ 5-epoch training on 20-row samples to verify model and API compatibility
       Input: data/ready/*.csv
       Output: outputs/smoke_4b/*.pkl

Phase 4B: Full Training (Fault-Tolerant)
  └─ 300-epoch CTGAN + PAR training on all 446,930 real rows
       Input: data/ready/*.csv + data/ready/metadata.json
       Output: outputs/models/*.pkl + outputs/synthetic/*.csv
                + SynthFHIR_checkpoint_after_*.zip (per table)
                + outputs/logs/manifest.json

Phase 5: Evaluation  [Planned]
  └─ Statistical fidelity (utility) and privacy risk quantification
       Input: data/ready/*.csv (real) + outputs/synthetic/*.csv (synthetic)
       Output: outputs/evaluation/ reports

Phase 6: FHIR Reconstruction  [Planned]
  └─ Convert synthetic CSVs back into FHIR JSON bundles
       Input: outputs/synthetic/*.csv
       Output: outputs/fhir_synthetic/*.json

Phase 7: Dashboard  [Planned]
  └─ Interactive Streamlit app comparing real and synthetic distributions
       Input: data/ready/*.csv + outputs/synthetic/*.csv + outputs/evaluation/
       Output: Running web application
```
